How the Market Auction Develops and Sentiment Analysis of Market Participants

What Is a Market Auction?

The market is a continuous process of asset exchange between participants, and the key mechanism governing this process is the market auction. It determines at what price financial instruments are bought and sold based on the balance of supply and demand.

Market auction theory is fundamental to all exchange trading. It explains how prices are formed through the actions of different types of participants â€“ from retail traders to institutional investors and market makers. This process unfolds in several stages, each influencing market dynamics.

Understanding the auction mechanism allows traders to:

  • Identify key support and resistance levels.
  • Find entry and exit points considering liquidity.
  • Assess the behavior of large players.
  • Detect areas of market balance and imbalance.

How Does the Market Transition from Balance to Imbalance?

The market constantly shifts between two phases:

  • Balance (Range): In this phase, the price trades within a narrow range as supply and demand are relatively equal.
  • Imbalance (Trend): When one side (buyers or sellers) takes control, the price begins to move in a single direction.

The alternation of these phases shapes market structure, which can be analyzed using auction theory tools such as Market Profile and volume analysis.

How Does the Market Auction Develop?

The market auction is a dynamic process in which price moves in response to the balance of supply and demand. It evolves through specific stages that help understand the current market phase and forecast future movements.

Main Stages of the Market Auction

  • Opening phase: The initial price is formed based on limit and market orders accumulated before the session begins.
  • Range formation phase: The market tests support and resistance levels, creating a balance zone.
  • Trend development phase: If supply or demand dominates, the price starts moving in one direction.
  • Culmination phase: Large players take profits, causing sharp reversals or consolidation.

This process can be observed using Market Profile â€“ a tool that shows where maximum liquidity is formed.

Balance and Imbalance in the Market Auction

The market constantly moves between balance and imbalance phases:

  • Balance phase (Accumulation): The price moves within a sideways range, as supply and demand are in equilibrium.
  • Imbalance phase (Trend): The dominance of one side leads to a strong upward or downward movement.

These phases form the foundation of the auction process and help traders identify areas where strong movements are likely.

How to Assess Market Participant Sentiment?

Market sentiment is the collective mood of traders and investors, reflecting their attitude toward the current market situation. Sentiment analysis helps identify crowd psychology, determine phases of fear and greed, and predict potential changes in the auction process.

Key Sentiment Indicators

  • Fear & Greed Index: Indicates whether the market is driven by panic selling or buying euphoria.
  • Trading volume (Volume Analysis) : High volumes during a price increase indicate bullish sentiment, while rising volumes during a decline suggest panic selling.
  • Put/Call Ratio: The ratio of call to put options, showing institutional sentiment.
  • Open interest structure: Futures data help identify key levels for large players.

How Does Sentiment Affect the Market Auction?

Sentiment analysis helps understand which emotions drive the market and how this influences auction dynamics:

  • Optimism phase: Participants actively buy, forming an uptrend.
  • Euphoria phase: The price reaches a peak, but volumes start declining â€“ a sign of a potential reversal.
  • Fear phase: Sharp sell-offs on high volumes indicate participant capitulation.
  • Equilibrium phase: After massive sell-offs, the market enters a sideways accumulation phase before a new movement.

How to Assess Market Sentiment and Use It in Trading?

Market sentiment is the collective mood of market participants, reflecting whether bullish or bearish sentiment is dominant. Sentiment analysis helps predict potential reversals and assess the strength of the current trend.

Key Market Sentiment Indicators

  • Fear & Greed Index: Indicates whether the market is dominated by fear (bearish sentiment) or greed (bullish sentiment).
  • Trading Volume: High volumes during an uptrend confirm bullish sentiment, while high volumes during a downtrend indicate growing bearish sentiment.
  • Open Interest: In futures and options, increasing open interest signals new money flowing in, strengthening the current trend.
  • Long/Short Ratio: If most traders are opening long positions, this may indicate overbought conditions.
  • Put/Call Ratio: High values indicate a bearish market, while low values suggest a bullish market.

How to Apply Sentiment Data in Trading?

Traders use sentiment analysis to spot anomalies and detect potential reversals:

  • Increasing volume during sideways price movement may indicate hidden position accumulation by large players.

  • A sharp increase in open interest near key levels signals strong institutional interest.

Recommendations for Using Sentiment Analysis

  • Use open position data to identify potential reversals.
  • Combine sentiment analysis with technical and volume analysis to improve entry accuracy.

How Do Market Makers Use Market Sentiment in Their Strategies?

Market makers and large institutional players actively use retail trader sentiment to manipulate the market. Their primary goal is to collect liquidity and create a supply and demand imbalance in their favor.

Key Market Maker Strategies

  • Creating false supply and demand: Large players place substantial orders in the order book that are quickly canceled. This creates the illusion of high demand or supply and forces retail traders to make incorrect decisions.
  • Stop-loss hunting: Market makers often move the price to stop-loss zones to collect liquidity before reversing in the opposite direction. This is known as "stop hunting."
  • False level breakouts: Before major moves, the price may temporarily break through a key level to trigger mass buying or selling, followed by a sharp reversal.
  • Exploiting news events: During high volatility periods, market makers may aggressively build positions, using crowd panic movements to their advantage.

How Do Market Makers Control the Auction Process?

The market auction develops through constant interaction between supply and demand. Market makers use auction mechanisms to identify areas of maximum interest and shift the price in the desired direction.

Examples of Manipulation:

  • High activity in the order book without actual trade execution â€“ creating artificial demand.
  • Sharp liquidity drops before major news releases â€“ preparation for a strong move.
  • "FOMO" (fear of missing out) manipulation â€“ pushing retail traders into impulsive trades.

How to Protect Yourself from Manipulation?

While retail traders cannot control the market, there are ways to reduce the likelihood of falling into market maker traps:

  • Avoid entering trades on impulses without confirmation signals.
  • Analyze volume â€“ false breakouts are often accompanied by low volume.
  • Monitor liquidity dynamics in the order book and tape prints.
  • Use POC (Point of Control) levels to identify real areas of institutional interest.
